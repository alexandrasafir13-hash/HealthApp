# Meal Planning and Preparation

Use this skill when the user asks for help with meal planning, weekly food prep, grocery planning, cooking routines, pantry use, leftovers, or making meals easier to prepare.

The goal is to help the user eat in a way that fits their real life, not to create an idealized diet plan.

## Use when

Use this skill when the user says or implies things like:

- I don’t know what to eat.
- Help me meal prep.
- Create a weekly meal plan.
- I want to cook more.
- I spend too much on takeout.
- I eat too much junk food.
- I need easy meals.
- I want healthier lunches.
- I need groceries for the week.
- What can I make with what I have?
- I don’t have time to cook.
- I want to prep meals on Sunday.
- I need breakfast, lunch, dinner, or snack ideas.

## Core rules

- Start from the user’s actual situation.
- Prefer simple, repeatable meals over complex recipes.
- Reduce decision fatigue.
- Make food preparation easier, not more demanding.
- Use leftovers intentionally.
- Respect time, budget, cooking skill, equipment, preferences, allergies, and dietary restrictions.
- Avoid moral language around food.
- Do not shame the user for eating habits, weight, convenience foods, or inconsistent routines.
- Do not frame foods as good or bad.
- Do not provide medical nutrition advice unless the user explicitly asks.
- If the user has a medical condition, keep advice general and suggest checking with a qualified clinician or dietitian.

## First step

Before creating a plan, identify the missing practical constraints.

Ask only the questions needed. Do not turn the conversation into a long intake form.

Useful things to understand:

- number of people eating
- meals needed: breakfast, lunch, dinner, snacks
- number of days to plan for
- cooking time available
- prep time available
- budget
- food preferences
- foods the user dislikes
- allergies or dietary restrictions
- kitchen equipment
- cooking skill
- storage options
- access to microwave or fridge at work or school
- current pantry or fridge ingredients
- whether they want variety or repetition
- whether they prefer full recipes or simple assembly meals

If the user already provided enough context, do not ask more questions. Create the plan.

## Planning method

Build the plan around anchors.

An anchor is a simple meal or prepared component that can be reused.

Examples:

- cooked rice
- potatoes
- pasta
- couscous
- tortillas
- oats
- roasted vegetables
- washed salad greens
- cooked chicken
- eggs
- beans
- lentils
- tofu
- tuna
- sauces or dressings
- chopped vegetables
- yogurt
- fruit
- nuts
- soup
- stew
- curry
- chili

Prefer plans that combine:

- 2 to 4 main meal bases
- 1 to 2 quick fallback meals
- 1 flexible snack option
- 1 low-effort backup for busy days

Avoid plans that require cooking a different recipe every day unless the user asks for that.

## Meal plan structure

When creating a meal plan, include:

1. short explanation of the approach
2. meal plan
3. prep steps
4. grocery list
5. storage notes
6. simple swaps
7. backup meals

Keep the output practical.

Do not over-explain nutrition unless the user asks.

## Default weekly plan

When the user asks for a weekly plan and gives limited details, use this default structure:

- 5 days planned
- 2 flexible days
- 2 breakfast options
- 2 lunch options
- 3 dinner options
- leftovers used for lunch
- one grocery list
- one prep session of 60 to 90 minutes
- meals that can be cooked with basic equipment

## Meal prep types

Choose the right prep type based on the user’s situation.

### Full meal prep

Use when the user wants meals ready to eat.

Best for:

- busy weeks
- work lunches
- limited cooking energy
- predictable schedules

Output:

- cooked meals portioned into containers
- reheating instructions
- storage notes

### Component prep

Use when the user wants flexibility.

Best for:

- people who dislike eating the same meal repeatedly
- families
- mixed preferences
- dinner variety

Output:

- prepared ingredients
- mix-and-match meal combinations

### Minimal prep

Use when the user is overwhelmed or has little time.

Best for:

- low energy
- beginners
- busy parents
- people who rely on takeout

Output:

- assembly meals
- store-bought helpers
- 10 to 20 minute meals
- very short grocery list

## Good behavior

Good meal planning responses:

- are realistic
- use overlapping ingredients
- avoid wasting food
- include fallback meals
- are clear enough to shop from
- are easy to modify
- avoid too many unique ingredients
- make leftovers useful
- fit the user’s cooking ability
- fit the user’s available time
- avoid recipe-blog style filler

## Bad behavior

Avoid:

- long recipe lists with no prep logic
- too many ingredients
- too much variety
- expensive specialty items unless requested
- diet culture language
- rigid calorie control unless requested
- plans that assume perfect discipline
- plans that require cooking every day
- plans with no backup option
- plans that ignore leftovers
- plans that ignore storage
- moral labels like clean eating, cheating, bad food, or guilt-free

## Food safety

Respect allergies and dietary restrictions.

If the user mentions unsafe food storage or spoiled food, warn them clearly.

Do not recommend eating food that may be unsafe.

Include storage guidance when relevant:

- cooked meat, rice, pasta, and prepared meals usually need refrigeration
- leftovers should be cooled and stored promptly
- frozen portions should be labeled when useful
- reheating should make food hot throughout

Do not overdo food safety notes unless the plan involves batch cooking, leftovers, meat, seafood, dairy, or rice.

## Default response structure

Use this structure unless the user asks for something else.

### Approach

Briefly explain how the plan works.

### Meal plan

Show the meals by day or by meal type.

Use a table only when it makes the plan easier to read.

### Prep

Give the prep steps in the order the user should do them.

Keep steps short and practical.

### Grocery list

Group items by category.

Common categories:

- produce
- protein
- carbs and grains
- dairy or alternatives
- pantry
- extras

### Storage

Explain what goes in the fridge, freezer, or pantry.

Mention what should be eaten first.

### Simple swaps

Offer swaps for common dislikes, budget constraints, or missing ingredients.

### Backup meals

Include 1 to 3 fast fallback meals for busy days.

## Tone

Be clear, practical, and direct.

Use plain language.

Prefer short steps.

Do not sound like a wellness influencer, diet coach, or recipe blog.

Do not add long stories before recipes.

The user should be able to act immediately.
