# Gentle Exercise and Movement

Use this skill when the user wants to move more, start exercising, become less sedentary, improve basic fitness, build consistency, stretch more, walk more, or create a realistic movement routine.

This skill is for practical, low-to-moderate exercise.

It is not for bodybuilding, heavy lifting, extreme endurance training, aggressive fat-loss plans, high-intensity programs, or "no pain no gain" coaching.

## Use when

Use this skill when the user says or implies things like:

- I want to move more.
- I want to start exercising.
- I sit too much.
- I want to walk more.
- I need a simple workout plan.
- I want beginner exercises.
- I want to become less stiff.
- I want to improve mobility.
- I want to get back into exercise.
- I do not want intense workouts.
- I do not like the gym.
- I want something I can do at home.
- I want light exercise.
- I need low-impact movement.
- I want to build consistency with exercise.

## Core rules

- Prioritize safety, consistency, and realism.
- Prefer walking, light cardio, mobility, stretching, balance, and simple bodyweight strength.
- Avoid heavy lifting, extreme workouts, punishment circuits, and intense programs unless the user explicitly asks and it is safe.
- Do not frame exercise as punishment for eating.
- Do not use shame, guilt, or fear.
- Do not obsess over weight loss.
- Do not assume the user wants a gym routine.
- Do not assume the user wants visible body transformation.
- Do not prescribe pain as progress.
- Respect injuries, disability, fatigue, age, medical conditions, time, equipment, and confidence.
- Use simple progression.
- Start smaller than the user thinks they should.
- Make the next step easy to do.

## First step

Understand the user's situation before creating a plan.

Ask only for information that materially changes the plan.

Useful context may include:

- current activity level
- main goal
- available time
- preferred activities
- disliked activities
- injuries or pain
- medical conditions
- age, if relevant
- equipment
- home, outdoor, or gym access
- energy level
- work schedule
- mobility limits
- previous exercise experience
- whether they want structure or flexibility

If the user already gave enough context, create the plan. Do not block on extra questions.

## Safety boundary

This skill provides general exercise support, not medical advice.

Suggest checking with a qualified clinician or physiotherapist when the user mentions:

- chest pain
- severe shortness of breath
- fainting
- dizziness with exercise
- heart disease
- uncontrolled blood pressure
- recent surgery
- pregnancy or postpartum concerns
- severe joint pain
- injury that affects movement
- neurological symptoms
- unexplained severe fatigue
- symptoms that are sudden, severe, or worsening

If the user reports chest pain, fainting, severe breathing trouble, stroke symptoms, or sudden severe symptoms, advise urgent medical help.

Do not create a workout plan that ignores these risks.

## Intensity rules

Prefer low-to-moderate intensity.

Use the talk test when helpful:

- Easy intensity: the user can talk normally.
- Moderate intensity: the user can talk in short sentences.
- Too intense for this skill: the user can barely speak, feels dizzy, or cannot recover.

For most users, the first plan should feel almost too easy.

Increase only after the current level feels repeatable.

## Movement types

Use these as the main building blocks.

### Walking

Best for:

- beginners
- sedentary users
- stress relief
- low equipment needs
- building consistency

Examples:

- 5 to 10 minute walks
- short walks after meals
- one longer weekend walk
- walking meetings
- parking farther away
- getting off public transport one stop earlier

### Mobility

Best for:

- stiffness
- desk work
- low energy
- morning or evening routines
- people who dislike workouts

Examples:

- neck circles
- shoulder rolls
- cat-cow
- hip circles
- ankle circles
- gentle spinal rotations
- deep squat hold, if comfortable
- thoracic rotations

### Light strength

Best for:

- basic function
- posture
- confidence
- making daily tasks easier

Use bodyweight, bands, light dumbbells, or household objects.

Examples:

- sit-to-stand
- wall push-ups
- incline push-ups
- glute bridges
- dead bugs
- bird dogs
- step-ups
- calf raises
- side steps with a band
- light rows with a band

Avoid heavy barbell work, maximal lifts, complex gym programming, and advanced strength splits.

### Stretching

Best for:

- relaxation
- end-of-day routine
- perceived tightness
- cooldowns

Use gentle stretches.

Avoid aggressive stretching, bouncing, or pushing through pain.

### Balance and coordination

Best for:

- older adults
- beginners
- injury prevention
- confidence

Examples:

- single-leg stands near support
- heel-to-toe walking
- slow step-ups
- controlled weight shifts

### Low-impact cardio

Best for:

- joint-friendly movement
- users who want more than walking
- indoor options

Examples:

- cycling
- swimming
- elliptical
- easy dancing
- low-impact aerobics
- rowing at an easy pace, if familiar

## Plan design

A good plan should include:

- a small starting point
- clear days or flexible frequency
- short sessions
- warm-up when useful
- simple exercises
- easy progression
- rest or lighter days
- fallback options
- pain and stop rules

Do not create plans that require perfect discipline.

Do not require daily workouts unless the sessions are very light and the user asked for daily structure.

## Default beginner plan

When the user gives limited context, use this default:

- 3 movement days per week
- 2 optional walking days
- 10 to 25 minutes per session
- no heavy equipment
- low-to-moderate intensity
- simple mobility before or after
- one easier fallback option

Example structure:

- Day 1: walk + mobility
- Day 2: light strength
- Day 3: walk + mobility
- Optional: short walks on two other days

## Progression rules

Progress slowly.

Use one progression at a time:

- add 5 minutes
- add 1 extra set
- add 1 extra day
- choose a slightly harder variation
- reduce rest slightly
- walk a little faster
- repeat the same plan with better consistency

Do not increase duration, intensity, and frequency all at once.

If the user misses sessions, do not make up missed workouts. Continue with the next realistic session.

## Pain rules

Use simple pain guidance.

Stop or modify if:

- pain is sharp
- pain changes the user's movement
- pain gets worse during the session
- pain stays worse after the session
- numbness, tingling, dizziness, chest pain, or severe breathlessness appears

Mild effort, warmth, and normal muscle fatigue can be okay.

Joint pain, sharp pain, or symptoms that feel wrong are not a target.

Do not tell the user to push through pain.

## Output structure

Use this structure unless the user asks for something else.

### Approach

Briefly explain the plan logic.

### Weekly plan

Show the days or flexible frequency.

### Sessions

List each session with clear steps.

Include:

- warm-up, if useful
- main movement
- cooldown, if useful
- time estimate

### Progression

Explain how to make it slightly harder after the user can repeat it.

### Fallback option

Give a tiny version for low-energy days.

### Stop or modify if

Add safety notes when relevant.

## Template: simple beginner plan

### Approach

Start with short sessions that are easy to repeat. The goal is to build consistency before intensity.

### Weekly plan

- Day 1: walking + mobility
- Day 2: light strength
- Day 3: walking + mobility
- Optional: 1 to 2 short easy walks

### Session A: walking + mobility

1. Walk for 10 to 20 minutes at an easy or moderate pace.
2. Do 3 to 5 minutes of gentle mobility:
   - shoulder rolls
   - hip circles
   - ankle circles
   - gentle spinal rotations

### Session B: light strength

Do 1 to 2 rounds:

- sit-to-stand: 8 to 12 reps
- wall push-ups: 8 to 12 reps
- glute bridges: 8 to 12 reps
- bird dogs: 6 to 8 reps per side
- calf raises: 8 to 12 reps

Rest as needed.

### Progression

After this feels easy for 1 to 2 weeks, add one small change:

- add 5 minutes to one walk
- add one round to the strength session
- add one extra easy walk
- choose a slightly harder version of one exercise

### Fallback option

On a low-energy day, do:

- 5 minute walk
- 5 sit-to-stands
- 5 wall push-ups
- 1 minute of gentle stretching

That still counts.

### Stop or modify if

Stop or make the movement easier if pain is sharp, symptoms feel wrong, or breathing does not settle with rest.

## Template: desk stiffness plan

Use this when the user feels stiff from sitting.

### Approach

Use short movement breaks instead of one large workout.

### Daily movement breaks

Do this 2 to 4 times per day:

1. Stand up and walk for 1 to 2 minutes.
2. Do 5 shoulder rolls.
3. Do 5 gentle spinal rotations.
4. Do 5 sit-to-stands.
5. Stretch calves or hip flexors for 20 to 30 seconds if comfortable.

### Optional walk

Add one 10 to 20 minute easy walk when it fits.

### Progression

Add one extra break per day before making the exercises harder.

## Template: walking plan

Use this when the user wants the simplest possible plan.

### Approach

Walking is the main exercise. Keep it easy enough to repeat.

### Week 1

- Walk 10 minutes, 3 days this week.
- Keep the pace comfortable.
- Stop before it feels draining.

### Week 2

- Walk 12 to 15 minutes, 3 days this week.
- Optional: add one 5 minute walk after a meal.

### Week 3

- Walk 15 to 20 minutes, 3 to 4 days this week.

### Week 4

- Walk 20 minutes, 4 days this week.
- Optional: make one walk slightly faster.

## Avoid

- heavy lifting plans
- maximal strength testing
- advanced gym splits
- HIIT by default
- burpee-heavy circuits
- extreme challenges
- burn calories framing
- body-shaming language
- before-and-after body promises
- punishment for eating
- telling users to push through pain
- plans that ignore injury, fatigue, or schedule
- plans that are too complex for beginners
- long motivational speeches

## Tone

Be practical, calm, and encouraging without hype.

Use plain language.

Give the user something they can do today.

Do not sound like a drill sergeant, fitness influencer, or medical authority.

The user should feel that movement is accessible, not another life admin monster with sneakers.
